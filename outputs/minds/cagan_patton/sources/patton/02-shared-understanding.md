# Jeff Patton: Shared Understanding vs. Shared Documents

**Source:** Various talks and writings (YOW, MBA016 podcast, presentations)
**Type:** Communication Philosophy
**Date Collected:** 2025-10-30
**Relevance:** Core collaboration principle

---

## Key Insight: Shared Documents ≠ Shared Understanding

One of Jeff Patton's most well-known insights is that "Shared documents aren't shared understanding."

When people share and sign off on documents, they might believe they understand although they have different interpretations. User story conversations help everyone arrive at shared understanding much faster than reading a requirements document.

## The Problem with Documents

Effective story conversations are about building shared understanding, and the best documents use words and pictures to help recall conversations—they don't replace them.

### Documentation as "Vacation Photos"

Jeff Patton uses the analogy that "What you record during conversations works like a vacation photo," helping you remember details that aren't in the photo.

Documents serve as memory aids for conversations already held, not as substitutes for the conversations themselves.

## The Role of Stories

The original idea of a story was simple: use it to facilitate a conversation, with stories having a simple lifecycle:
1. **Card** - Write it down
2. **Conversation** - Talk about it
3. **Confirmation** - Agree on how you'll know it's done

Stories are not specifications; they are conversation prompts.

## Story Conversations

User story conversations help everyone arrive at shared understanding much faster than reading a requirements document. The conversation is where understanding happens—the card is just a reminder that we need to have it.

## Building Shared Understanding

Jeff Patton shows how to use Story Maps to:
- Create a shared understanding of a feature
- Create thin slices that relate to the minimum viable product
- Plan additional releases
- Break down big stories and help find small viable solutions

Story maps are used to understand your product or feature's experience, not just to document it.

## DNA Mental™ Layer Markers

**Layer 1 - Mental Model:** Conversation as understanding mechanism, documents as memory aids
**Layer 2 - Thought Pattern:** Collaborative construction over document review
**Layer 3 - Language:** Simple analogies ("vacation photos"), accessible metaphors
**Layer 4 - Decision Architecture:** Conversation first, documentation second
**Layer 5 - Values:** Shared understanding > Documentation; Conversation > Specification
**Layer 6 - Obsession:** Making invisible understanding visible through conversation
**Layer 8 - Paradox:** Documents are necessary but insufficient (memory aids, not understanding transfer)
