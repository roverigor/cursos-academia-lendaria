# Tension: Why Product Development Requires Balancing Conflicting Goals

**Author**: Jeff Patton
**Date**: November 2022
**Collected**: 2025-10-30
**Type**: Article

## Introduction

This article explores the fundamental tensions in product development between seemingly opposing forces: business objectives and customer needs, project delivery and product success, and organizational sustainability versus user empowerment.

## The Core Framework

The author describes product development using four interconnected concepts:

- **Opportunity**: Customer problems or unmet needs discovered through research
- **Output**: The work teams perform to create products and features
- **Outcome**: How customers respond—what they do, say, and feel after using the product
- **Impact**: Business benefits like ROI, brand awareness, and market share

## Key Tensions Identified

### Business Problems ≠ Customer Problems

Organizations cannot directly solve their own challenges. Instead, they must identify customer opportunities aligned with business strategy. As the article notes: "Your business can't directly solve its problems. It needs customers to solve them."

Business goals—meeting financial targets, increasing market share—differ fundamentally from customer goals. Customers don't care about your organization's ROI or market position.

### Output ≠ Outcome

Delivering features on schedule and within budget means nothing if customers don't adopt or value them. The author's early experience building online ordering software illustrates this: delivering on requirements while creating a worse user experience than existing alternatives.

### Efficiency Doesn't Equal Success

"On time delivery doesn't lead to customer or business success." Speed and scope management matter less than understanding whether customers actually benefit from what's delivered.

### Customer Value ≠ Business Value

These operate through different mechanisms:
- Customers derive value through direct use and problem-solving
- Organizations earn value through monetizing that customer adoption

## The Danger of Imbalance

### Focusing Solely on Business Goals

When organizations prioritize business metrics without ensuring customer value, unethical behaviors emerge. Examples include:

- **Wells Fargo**: Employees opened unauthorized accounts to meet targets, ignoring customer harm
- **Facebook/Cambridge Analytica**: Harvested user data for profit, harming one customer segment to benefit another

The author warns: "You can create business value without creating customer value – but only until you get caught."

### Focusing Solely on Customer Demands

While empathy is valuable, fulfilling every customer request creates problems:

- Product complexity increases, harming other users
- Organizations support features indefinitely, reducing ROI
- Individual customizations eliminate the leverage that justifies product investment
- Users often don't use capabilities they requested

Pursuing customer success alone leads to what the author calls "ruinous empathy."

## The Motivation Problem

Drawing from Dan Pink's *Drive*, the author identifies three motivation factors:

1. **Autonomy**: Self-direction and decision-making authority
2. **Mastery**: Continuous improvement and skill development
3. **Purpose**: Making the world better

The critical insight: "When the profit motive comes unhinged from the purpose motive, bad things happen."

Building products that genuinely help people—where customer success drives business success—creates genuine motivation. Generic business metrics and stakeholder management don't.

## Managing the Tensions

Success requires acknowledging all tensions simultaneously:

- Organizations must find customer opportunities that align with strategy
- Delivery speed matters only if outcomes demonstrate customer value
- Business sustainability depends on customer value creation
- Team motivation comes from connecting individual work to customer impact

The resolution isn't choosing one side; it's recognizing that customer success and business success are interdependent.

---

## Summary

Product development inherently contains tensions between competing goods: business viability and customer benefit, delivery efficiency and outcome quality, individual customer requests and product sustainability. Organizations thrive by navigating these tensions consciously rather than favoring one pole exclusively.
